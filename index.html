MB
Michael B. CEO & Co-Founder · Oncology Startup, Boston
Founder Life
I watched a biotech founder break down on a Zoom call last week. Not because the science failed. Not because funding ran out. Because their co-founder — their best friend of 8 years — had just sent a calm email: "I think it's time I move on." They sat there in their lab coat. Quiet. Then said: "I don't even know if I'm building this for the right reasons anymore." I didn't have an answer. But I've heard this from too many founders to think it's rare. The science can be brilliant. The market can be real. The team can be talented. And still — the hardest thing about building a biotech startup isn't any of that. It's staying connected to your WHY when everything around you is pulling you apart. If you've been there — what brought you back? 👇 Tell me below. More founders need to hear this.
SP
Sarah P. Founder · Drug Discovery Platform, SF
Fundraising
My first cold email to a VC got 0 replies. Subject: "Revolutionary cancer treatment — can we talk?" I thought it was perfect. So I rewrote it. And rewrote it. And rewrote it again. 11 versions over 3 weeks. Version 11 didn't start with the science. It didn't mention TAM, SAM, or SOM. It didn't even mention my credentials. It started with a single line about a real patient. A 43-year-old teacher named Maria — who was the reason I started this. 3 VCs replied within 48 hours. Here's what no one tells you about biotech fundraising: Investors don't fund science. They fund belief. And the fastest way to make someone believe in your science is to show them the human on the other side of it. What's the one story from your work that made YOU believe it was worth everything? 👇 Drop it below — I genuinely want to know.
JK
James K. Founder · Cell Therapy Startup, NYC
Team Building
I passed on a Stanford PhD. Hired someone who learned pipetting from YouTube instead. My board thought I'd lost my mind. 6 months later — that person found a contamination issue in our cell culture process that three senior scientists had missed for weeks. Why? Because they weren't afraid to ask "stupid" questions. Because they hadn't yet learned to assume things. Because they were still curious in the way only beginners can be. The most dangerous person in your lab isn't the one with the least credentials. It's the one who stopped asking why. Your first 5 hires shape your company culture more than any values doc you ever write. Hire people who lose sleep over the same problem you do. Credentials are a starting point. Curiosity is the whole game. What's the best "unconventional" hire you've ever made? 👇 I'll feature the best story in my next post.
RA
Riya A. Co-Founder · Diagnostics Startup, Cambridge
Science, Simplified
Scientists just turned CRISPR into a pregnancy test. For cancer. No, I'm not exaggerating. You pee on a strip. It detects cancer DNA in your urine. In 20 minutes. At home. Under $5. Here's the wild part — the same protein we've been using to cut DNA? They reprogrammed it to detect it instead. When it finds a cancer mutation, it sends a fluorescent signal. That signal shows up on the strip. No hospital. No waiting room. No $3,000 scan. Just a strip. A bathroom. And an answer. This is what happens when biotech stops trying to be impressive and starts trying to be useful. The next billion-dollar breakthrough in diagnostics won't come from a bigger machine. It'll come from a founder who asks: "How do we make this fit in a pocket?" Are you working on something that makes complex science simple? 👇 Tell me about it — this kind of work deserves more attention.
